**Algorithmic Fairness Metrics and Bias Types** are mathematical frameworks and conceptual classifications used to diagnose, measure, and mitigate unintended harms in machine learning models. Because statistical models learn patterns directly from data, they risk automating and magnifying human prejudices or sampling flaws if left unchecked.

### Types of Algorithmic Bias
* **Disparate Treatment vs. Disparate Impact**: Disparate treatment occurs when a model explicitly utilizes a **sensitive attribute** (e.g., race, gender, age) to make a prediction or classification. Disparate impact is a more insidious harm, occurring when apparently neutral features act as **proxies** for sensitive attributes (e.g., using postal codes as a proxy for race or income), resulting in disproportionately negative outcomes for specific subgroups.
* **Unawareness**: The practice of simply omitting sensitive attributes from a training dataset. This fails to guarantee fairness because deep models easily reconstruct the omitted attributes from other highly correlated proxy variables in the data.
* **Automation Bias**: A cognitive bias where human decision-makers over-rely on recommendations generated by an automated system, ignoring evidence of errors or their own clinical/professional judgment.
* **Selection Bias**: Flaws in data collection that yield non-representative datasets, categorized into **coverage bias** (the sample doesn't match the target population), **sampling bias** (non-random collection), and **non-response/participation bias** (certain subgroups opting out of feedback or surveys at higher rates).

### Formal Fairness Metrics
* **Demographic Parity**: Requires the probability of a positive outcome to be identical across all subgroups (e.g., accepting equal percentages of applicants from two different demographics), regardless of qualifications.
* **Equality of Opportunity**: Ensures the model is equally effective at predicting desirable outcomes across subgroups. Specifically, the **true positive rate** (TPR) must be identical across sensitive attributes (e.g., qualified candidates of all groups have the same chance of admission).
* **Equalized Odds**: A stricter criterion requiring both the true positive rates (TPR) and the false positive rates (FPR) to be equal across all subgroups under consideration.
* **Counterfactual Fairness**: Assesses whether a classification model produces the identical outcome for an individual as it would if one or more of their sensitive attributes were counterfactually changed.

### The Incompatibility of Fairness Metrics
A fundamental mathematical reality is the **incompatibility of fairness metrics**. In non-trivial real-world datasets, it is mathematically impossible to simultaneously satisfy demographic parity, equalized odds, and predictive parity (except in highly idealized scenarios where base rates of qualifications are perfectly identical across all groups). AI project design and governance teams must therefore make explicit, value-driven trade-offs depending on the legal and social context of the deployment.
